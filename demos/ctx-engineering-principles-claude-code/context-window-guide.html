§ 01

What Is a Context Window?

Source: Softwareguru, July 2025 ↗

A context window is the maximum amount of context — measured in tokens — an LLM can process and remember at any given time. It's the model's working memory: everything in it at once includes your system prompt, conversation history, uploaded documents, tool outputs, and the model's own prior replies.

Token Math
Roughly 75 words = 100 tokens. A dense research paper is ~10,000–30,000 tokens. A long working session can easily cross 50,000–100,000 tokens. Modern models: Claude at 200K, Gemini at 1M.

§ 02

Context Rot — The Core Problem

Source: Chroma Research, July 2025 · 18 models · 194,480 LLM calls ↗

Chroma Research tested 18 state-of-the-art models — Claude Opus 4, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5, Qwen3, and others — on controlled tasks across increasing input lengths. Their finding: model performance degrades as input length increases, often in surprising and non-uniform ways, even on tasks as simple as copying text.

Three mechanisms drive context rot, and they compound each other:

Mechanism 01

Lost-in-the-Middle Effect

Models attend most strongly to tokens at the very beginning and very end, and poorly to everything in between. The U-shaped performance curve.

Mechanism 02

Attention Dilution

Transformer self-attention tracks every token-to-token relationship. More tokens = exponentially more relationships = diluted attention per token.

Mechanism 03

Distractor Interference

Semantically similar but irrelevant content causes degradation beyond what context length alone explains. This is the worst kind of noise.

Attention Dilution — Pairwise Relationships
As context grows, attention must track exponentially more token-to-token relationships. Per-token attention weight shrinks toward zero.
Performance Drop at 32K Tokens (Adobe Study, Feb 2025)
Single-hop reasoning accuracy on short context vs. 32K-token context. Models that score near 100% on short prompts degrade dramatically.
Hover bars for details
Critical Finding
A model with a 1M token context window still exhibits context rot at 50K tokens. The problem starts the moment you add more content than needed — not when you approach the limit.
Cross-Lab Confirmation
Google DeepMind found that trimming irrelevant content is always beneficial for model accuracy. Microsoft and Salesforce reported accuracy dropping from 90% to 51% in multi-turn dialogues as conversations grew longer.
§ 03

Lost in the Middle

Source: Liu et al., Stanford / TACL 2024 ↗

The landmark "Lost in the Middle" paper showed that performance is highest when relevant information occurs at the beginning or end of the input context, and significantly degrades when models must access relevant information in the middle — even for models designed for long contexts.

The Drop
LLMs degrade by more than 30% when relevant information sits in the middle rather than at the start or end. For GPT-3.5, performance drops from 75.8% (position 0) to 53.8% (position 9 of 20) — falling below the closed-book baseline.

Needle in a Haystack Test (Kamradt, 2023)

A single fact was embedded in Paul Graham essays (750 to 96,000 words) at varying positions. Results: 100% retrieval accuracy under 64K tokens, with performance degrading beyond that — especially when the fact appeared in the middle. When inserted as the first sentence, accuracy held regardless of document length.

Actionable Rule
Put your most important content and instructions first, not last and definitely not in the middle. If providing a document, place your question before the document.
§ 04

Types of Hallucination

Source: Alansari & Luqman, Comprehensive Survey, arXiv Oct 2025 ↗

Hallucination is the generation of content that is fluent and syntactically correct, but factually inaccurate or unsupported by evidence. Understanding the type helps you know which risks apply to your use case. Click each type to expand.

Type 1

Intrinsic Hallucination

+

The model generates content that directly contradicts information in the source material. This is the most dangerous type — the model actively overwrites ground truth with fabricated content.

Example
Source says: "The study was conducted by Dr. Sarah Chen." Model outputs: "The study was conducted by Dr. James Liu." — the model replaced a correct fact with a fabricated one.
Detection Strategy
Explicitly compare every named entity and key fact in the output against your source material. Contradictions are easy to miss without systematic checking.
Type 2

Extrinsic Hallucination

+

The model adds information not in the source, but doesn't contradict it either. This is harder to detect because the additions sound plausible and are consistent with the document's tone.

Example
Source: a research abstract about a protein discovery. Model adds: "The research was funded by the NIH and published in Nature Medicine" — neither claim is in the abstract, both sound credible.
Detection Strategy
After receiving a summary, ask: "List every claim you made. For each, cite the exact sentence from the source that supports it." Unsupported claims surface quickly.
Type 3

Factuality Hallucination

+

Divergence between the model's output and known real-world facts. Most common on niche topics, recent events, and specific statistics that appear rarely or inconsistently in training data.

Example
"The Eiffel Tower was built in 1892" (it was 1889). Or inventing a paper citation: "Smith et al., 2019, showed that..." — the paper doesn't exist, but the citation format is perfect.
Detection Strategy
Treat all specific dates, statistics, citations, and niche facts as unverified until confirmed against a primary source. Never use model-generated citations without checking they exist.
Type 4

Faithfulness Hallucination

+

The model fails to follow your instructions or stay consistent with context you provided. This is a failure of instruction-following, not factual recall — and it increases significantly as context length grows.

Example
You said "respond only in French." Mid-way through a long conversation, it starts responding in English. Or: "Do not mention competitors" — but after 40 messages, it forgets and names them.
Detection Strategy
For long tasks, repeat critical constraints at the start of each new message. If a session exceeds ~20 exchanges, start fresh and re-state your key instructions.
The Sycophancy Amplifier
All four types are worsened by RLHF training dynamics. Models are trained to sound confident and to match what users seem to want — leading to hallucinated responses delivered with certainty. The model has been optimized to be convincing, not just correct.
§ 05

Why Hallucination Cannot Be Fully Eliminated

Source: Xu et al., arXiv 2024/2025 — A Formal Proof ↗

Xu et al. formally proved using learning theory that LLMs cannot learn all computable functions and will therefore inevitably hallucinate if used as general problem solvers. This is not an engineering flaw that better training will eventually fix — it is a mathematical property of the learning framework.

Key Takeaway
Hallucination cannot be eliminated. It can only be managed and reduced. The goal of any trust framework is to minimize probability and severity — not to achieve certainty. Build for graceful degradation, not for perfect reliability.
OpenAI's Framing (December 2025)
Standard training and evaluation reward guessing over acknowledging uncertainty. Models are scored on accuracy, so guessing has positive expected value while saying "I don't know" scores zero. This creates systematic overconfidence baked into the training process itself — independent of model capability.

The Asymmetry You Need to Understand

  • A confident answer that's wrong looks identical to a confident answer that's right
  • Models are penalized for saying "I don't know" during training — so they rarely do
  • More capable models may produce more convincing hallucinations, not fewer
  • The solution is always verification systems, not model trust
§ 06

The Context Management Playbook

A practical framework grounded in the research above. Click each rule to mark it as a habit. Track your progress.

Rules adopted 0 / 7
01

Keep Context Lean and Intentional

The optimal context window in practice is often much smaller than the technical maximum. Before uploading a document, ask: is this entire document needed, or just the relevant sections? Test: read your prompt as if you're the model — remove anything not needed.

✓
02

Front-Load What Matters

Place your most critical instructions, constraints, and context at the beginning of your prompt. Based on Lost-in-the-Middle findings: place your question before the document, not after. Structure: Question → Key constraints → Supporting material.

✓
03

Start a New Chat for a New Task

Long chat histories are noise the model processes on every message. An unrelated old conversation is pure distractor interference. Heuristic: if your chat has crossed ~20 messages or ~30–40 minutes of active work, open a fresh session.

✓
04

Ground the Model in Source Material

Hallucination risk drops when the model does extraction from provided text rather than relying on parametric memory. RAG systems can reduce hallucination rates by 60–80% by grounding responses in verified documents. Paste the source; then ask.

✓
05

Watch for Extrinsic Additions

The most common failure when working from documents is not contradicting the source — it's adding to it (Type 2 hallucination). Always ask: "Is every claim in this response actually supported by what I provided?"

✓
06

Use Structure to Reduce Ambiguity

Chain-of-thought prompting, asking the model to cite where each claim came from, and requesting it flag uncertainty all measurably improve output reliability in research on prompt sensitivity.

✓
07

Push Back on Confident Answers

After a confident answer, ask: "What would be a reason this answer could be wrong?" or "What assumptions did you make?" A model that hallucinates confidently will often reveal uncertainty under gentle challenge.

✓
§ 07

Trust Calibration by Task Type

Not all LLM tasks carry the same hallucination risk. Filter by risk level to focus on what matters for your context.

Task Type Risk Why Trust Strategy
Summarizing a provided document Low Grounded in source, extractive Check for extrinsic additions
Q&A from a provided document Low Grounded, verifiable Spot-check key claims against source
Research synthesis across sources Medium Multiple sources = conflict risk Ask model to cite which source supports each claim
Generating from memory (no source) High Purely parametric recall Treat as starting point; verify independently
Specific facts: dates, citations, stats High Sparse training data = high fabrication rate Always verify against primary sources
Long conversations (50+ messages) Med–High Context rot degrades instruction-following Refresh context or start new chat
Creative / brainstorming tasks Low–Med Factual accuracy less critical Define scope of what needs to be factual
